# Course Design Analysis: HCI 3900 — Human Computer Interaction (2026)

*Analysis generated April 2026. Covers all 12 lectures, 4 assessments, 10 workshops, and resource materials.*

---

## Overview

This is a well-structured, ambitious course with a genuine research orientation that's unusual at this level. The scaffolded assessment design, creative prompts, and strong alignment between lectures, workshops, and assessments are real achievements. What follows is honest feedback on both what's working well and where further attention would strengthen the course.

---

## Strengths

**1. Backward design is genuinely executed, not just claimed**

The alignment between what's taught, what's practiced, and what's assessed is tight and evident. Workshop 4 (interviews) directly scaffolds Assignment 2; Workshop 6 (thematic analysis) directly feeds into the final project. Students can see why they're doing each thing. This is harder to achieve than it looks.

**2. Research-as-practice is embedded throughout**

From Week 1, students are writing in ACM format, citing sources, and engaging with conference proceedings. Workshop 8 asks them to analyse actual CHI and DIS papers against methodological criteria. Workshop 10 requires synthesising across papers to form implications. This is genuine exposure to how the discipline works, not a gesture toward it. The research clusters for Assignment 2 are an elegant structural move — participants who understand the context make the data richer and the ethics simpler.

**3. The assessment prompts are excellent**

Animal-Computer Interaction is genuinely novel and forces students to interrogate assumptions about users, context, and design. The Sustainable Living + non-standard interaction constraint for the final project is well-chosen: it's grounded, consequential, and pushes against keyboard-mouse defaults.

**4. Qualitative methods receive serious treatment**

Two full lectures (Weeks 5 and 6) plus multiple workshops on thematic analysis, reflexivity, and positionality is more depth than most HCI courses at this level. Workshop 10's multimedia coding exercise with the Tuvalu photo series is particularly strong — it builds the analytical skill in a different modality before applying it to interaction data.

**5. Pre-class tasks create repeated low-stakes engagement**

Weekly forum posts with references mean students are reading, thinking, and writing about HCI every week. This generates genuine cumulative exposure to the material that a single assignment cycle wouldn't achieve.

## Areas Worth Examining

### Sequencing Issues

**The quantitative statistics lecture (Week 10) comes after Assignment 2 is due (Week 9).**

This is the most structurally significant problem. Students are asked to collect and analyse quantitative data for Assignment 2 — run SUS surveys, compute statistics, interpret findings — before the statistical analysis lecture that would support them doing this well. Workshop 5 introduces basic SUS analysis, which partially covers the gap, but students designing their research plans (Workshop 7, Week 7) are making decisions about quantitative methods without the theoretical grounding from Lecture 10.

The fix could be to move the statistical content to Week 5 or 6, or restructure so Assignment 2 is due in Week 10-11. The current sequence asks students to swim before teaching them to float.

**Research skills (Google Scholar, citation) are taught in Week 7, but required from Week 1.**

Pre-class posts from Week 1 require ≥2 ACM-format references, and Assignment 1 (due Week 5) requires the same. Lecture 7 is the first explicit coverage of using Google Scholar and academic citation. Many students will muddle through, but this creates an invisible barrier for students who haven't encountered academic referencing before. A brief introduction to research skills in Week 1 or 2 would reduce early anxiety.

**Human-Centred AI (Week 12) arrives after students have already completed a study of AI interfaces.**

Assignment 2 is centred on AI-integrated interfaces and due in Week 9. The theoretical frameworks for thinking about human-AI interaction arrive in Week 12, after the assignment is submitted. Students are doing user research on AI without the conceptual vocabulary the course provides for understanding it. Even a brief framing of human-AI interaction in Weeks 5-6, when the research topic is introduced, would help significantly.

---

### Balance and Repeated Exposure

**Cognitive, social, and emotional interaction (Lecture 9) is treated as a block rather than a thread.**

These ideas — cognitive load, distributed cognition, affordances, social presence, emotional design — are foundational to everything in the course, but they're gathered into a single lecture in Week 9, after students have already designed prototypes and conducted research. These concepts would be more powerful if introduced early (Weeks 1-2) and revisited explicitly as students engage with design and evaluation. The current placement means students may complete their prototyping work without access to theoretical language that would sharpen their design rationale.

**The "Interfaces" lecture (Week 7) covers a wide sweep without clear analytical throughline.**

This lecture reviews many interface types — tangible, voice, wearable, and so on — arriving in Week 7 after students have already completed Assignment 1. The content reads somewhat like a catalog. Framing it around an explicit analytical question — "what tradeoffs do different interface paradigms make, and how do you justify the choice?" — would make it more generative for the final project.

---

### Explicit Teaching of Core Theory

**The course is rich in methods but lighter on theoretical frameworks.**

Excellent coverage of research methods (interviewing, surveys, thematic analysis, evaluation design). The theoretical depth — Activity Theory, Situated Action, Distributed Cognition, GOMS, Norman's action cycle — is present but thin. Students need conceptual handles for interpreting what they find. At the moment, the main theoretical touchstone is Norman's design principles (implied throughout) and the cognitive/social/emotional categories in Lecture 9. A more explicit theoretical spine — even three or four frameworks introduced early and consistently referred back to — would strengthen students' ability to write research rationales and interpret findings.

---

### Postgraduate Differentiation

**The PG differentiation is thin.**

COMP6390 students are distinguished primarily by the positionality statement in the final project (5 marks). For students enrolled at postgraduate level, this may feel insufficient. Options worth considering: a more demanding literature review component, a methodological justification section, or requiring engagement with a greater number of primary sources. The positionality requirement is good and substantively important, but a modest differentiator.

---

### Small but Worth Noting

**Word limits are tight given the complexity expected.** 1500 words for Assignment 1's design rationale — covering task/users, design process, prototype features, and references — is challenging for students learning to write in an academic register. 1800 words for a full research study with both quantitative and qualitative strands is also constrained. Slightly more generous limits, or clearer guidance on proportioning sections, might reduce stress without reducing rigor.

**The final project video format is bold and interesting**, but accessibility deserves attention — students with anxiety about self-presentation, with non-native English, or without reliable equipment may face different challenges than the rubric accounts for.

---

## Summary Priorities

Three things to address first:

1. **Move the stats lecture earlier**, or adjust Assignment 2's due date — this is the clearest structural misalignment.
2. **Introduce brief HCAI framing when Assignment 2 is introduced** — students need some theoretical context for what they're studying.
3. **Distribute foundational cognitive/social theory across the first few weeks** rather than concentrating it in Week 9 — it would lift the quality of everything students produce.
